Risk Rule Optimization Project Interview Prep Card — Same-card ban soft delete experiment

Problem Statement

The existing rule

Same-card ban: if User A registers with Credit Card X, then User B cannot register with the same Credit Card X
Intent: block fraudsters reactivating under a new user ID after being banned
Unintended consequence: legitimate churned users who want to reactivate are blocked — their card is "on file" from their old account

The tension

Fraud prevention goal: block bad actors from creating multiple accounts with one card
Growth goal: allow legitimate returning customers to reactivate without friction
These two goals directly conflict — the same rule that blocks fraud also blocks genuine reactivation
Hypothesis & Solution

Root cause analysis

When a user churns, their card information is kept on file (hard delete never happens)
This means the card remains "claimed" by the churned account forever
Any new account with the same card — even the same real person — is blocked

Proposed solution

Soft delete card on churn: when a user churns, mark their card as soft-deleted (not hard-deleted)
Soft-deleted card no longer blocks same-card ban rule during reactivation
Downstream risk checks (Omni score, AVS, chargeback rules) still run — bad actors get caught by other signals
"We hypothesised that soft-deleting card information on churn would unblock legitimate reactivation without meaningfully increasing fraud, because downstream risk checks would catch bad actors who previously relied on card anonymity."
Experiment Design

Setup

Duration: 4 weeks
Unit: User (reactivation attempt)
Treatment: soft-delete card on churn → unblock reactivation
Control: existing same-card ban rule (hard block)
Population: churned users attempting reactivation with a previously-used card

Success metrics

Primary: Net revenue (revenue minus fraud losses)
Conversion: Onboard activation rate
Safety: Chargeback rate
Safety: ATO (Account Takeover) report rate
Downstream: Omni risk score distribution
Downstream: AVS (Address Verification) check pass rate

Risk mitigations pre-launch

Downstream risk checks remain active for treatment group
Omni score threshold set conservatively for reactivating users
Real-time chargeback monitoring with auto-pause if spike detected
Legal/Compliance pre-review of rule change
Gradual rollout: 5% → 20% → 50% → 100%
Results (4-week experiment)

Net revenue

Positive

Primary metric — significant

Onboard activation rate

+5 BPS

~1,000 MTM incremental users

Downstream risk blocks

Active

Bad actors caught by Omni/AVS

What worked

Net revenue positive — legitimate reactivators generated incremental GMV
+5 BPS activation rate = ~1,000 more activated users per month
Downstream risk checks (Omni score, AVS) successfully filtered bad actors who tried to exploit the relaxed rule
Chargeback rate and ATO rate held stable — no meaningful fraud uplift

Key insight

The same-card ban was over-blocking: it was rejecting many legitimate returning customers
Downstream risk signals (Omni, AVS) are more precise than a blanket card-level ban
Soft delete is the right data lifecycle decision — permanent card retention creates false blocks
Layered defense works better than a single blunt rule
Metrics Deep Dive — What Each One Measures

Why net revenue is the right North Star

Revenue alone would miss fraud cost — a bad actor completing onboarding looks like revenue
Net revenue = revenue − expected fraud losses (chargebacks + ATO losses)
Positive net revenue means the incremental legitimate users outweighed incremental fraud
This is the right framing for any risk-relaxation experiment: the question is always net value, not gross conversion

Guardrail metric logic

Chargeback rate: direct cost signal — if fraudsters reactivate and dispute charges, this spikes first
ATO report rate: account takeover reports — signals if stolen credentials are being reused via the relaxed rule
Omni score: downstream composite risk score — validates that relaxed entry-point is offset by tighter downstream
AVS check: address verification — catches stolen cards where billing address doesn't match
Interview Soundbites
"The core insight was that a single blunt rule — same card, same ban — was doing too much work. It was trying to be both an identity check and a fraud signal. By soft-deleting cards on churn, we separated those two concerns: card identity no longer persists after churn, and downstream risk checks handle the fraud signal more precisely."
"Net revenue was the right primary metric because in risk-relaxation experiments, gross conversion is misleading. A fraudster who gets through looks like revenue until the chargeback. Net revenue forces you to account for fraud losses in the same metric, so the experiment can only succeed if legitimate users genuinely outweigh bad actors."
"The downstream risk checks weren't just guardrails — they were the enabler. The reason we could relax the entry-point rule was specifically because we had Omni score, AVS, and chargeback monitoring as a second layer. Without those, we couldn't have justified the relaxation. That's the layered defence principle: you can afford to be less blunt at the top of the funnel if you're more precise downstream."
Anticipated Follow-up Questions
Q: How did you handle the fact that fraud signals are lagged? Chargebacks can take 30-90 days to materialise. We set a conservative monitoring window and used leading indicators (AVS fail rate, velocity checks on new activations) as early signals before the 4-week experiment window closed.
Q: Why soft delete and not hard delete? Hard delete would destroy the historical association permanently. Soft delete preserves the audit trail — if a user later files a dispute or if we need to investigate fraud, the card linkage history is still queryable.
Q: What is 5 BPS in context? 5 basis points = 0.05% improvement in activation rate. At the scale of reactivation attempts, that translates to ~1,000 incremental activated users per month (MTM). Over a year, that's ~12,000 users and the associated revenue.
Q: What would cause you to roll back? Three stopping criteria: chargeback rate exceeds 1.5x baseline, ATO report rate exceeds 2x baseline, or Omni score distribution for treatment group shifts meaningfully toward high-risk bucket.
Q: How did you validate the experiment wasn't contaminated by seasonal effects? 4 weeks spans multiple billing cycles and controls for weekly seasonality. We also ran a pre-experiment AA test to confirm baseline comparability between treatment and control populations.
